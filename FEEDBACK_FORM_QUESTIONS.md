# Tester Feedback Form

Copy these questions into a Google Form (forms.google.com → Blank). The structure is designed to surface honest feedback, not polite agreement.

For each question, I've noted what TYPE of question it should be in Google Forms (e.g., "short answer," "paragraph," "linear scale 1-5") and WHY each question matters. Don't include the "why" notes in the actual form — those are just for you.

---

## Section 1: First Impressions

### Question 1
**The first screen — the welcome with the disclaimer and the "come sit with us" button. What was your first reaction?**
*(Paragraph)*

[Why: First impressions reveal whether the tone and the legal language coexist or conflict. This is where you find out if the disclaimer feels heavy enough to be safe but light enough to not feel clinical.]

### Question 2
**Did the welcome screen feel like Swift Steps?**
*(Linear scale 1-5: 1 = "felt like a generic app," 5 = "felt unmistakably like Swift Steps")*

[Why: Numerical answer makes it easy to spot consensus or disagreement across testers.]

### Question 3
**If you said anything below a 4 on the previous question — what was missing or off?**
*(Paragraph, optional)*

---

## Section 2: The Companion Chat

### Question 4
**What did you say to the companion?**
*(Paragraph)*

[Why: Tells you whether they engaged with something real or just kicked the tires. Real engagement means the design is working.]

### Question 5
**Did the companion sound like Julianne, or did it sound like a chatbot?**
*(Multiple choice: "Sounded like Julianne" / "Mostly like Julianne, but a few moments off" / "Sounded like a chatbot trying to be Julianne" / "Sounded like a chatbot")*

[Why: Forces a specific judgment instead of a polite "it was good." This is the most important question in the whole form.]

### Question 6
**Was there a specific reply where you thought "yes, that's exactly how she'd say it"?**
*(Paragraph)*

[Why: Captures what's working so we don't accidentally tune away from it.]

### Question 7
**Was there a specific reply where you thought "she would never say that"?**
*(Paragraph)*

[Why: This is the gold. The off-moments tell us exactly where to refine the system prompt.]

### Question 8
**If you were having a hard night at 11pm, would you actually open this?**
*(Multiple choice: "Yes, definitely" / "Maybe, depends on the kind of hard" / "Probably not" / "Definitely not")*

[Why: Cuts through politeness. "It's nice but I wouldn't use it" is critical information.]

### Question 9
**If you said maybe or no — what would have to be true for you to actually open it?**
*(Paragraph, optional)*

---

## Section 3: The Rest Of The App

### Question 10
**Did you check out the reflect tab (the journaling prompts)? If so, what did you think?**
*(Paragraph)*

### Question 11
**Did you do a daily check-in? What was that experience like?**
*(Paragraph)*

### Question 12
**Anything in the app that felt confusing, broken, or like it was missing?**
*(Paragraph)*

[Why: Catches usability issues you can't see when you've been staring at it for weeks.]

---

## Section 4: The Bigger Picture

### Question 13
**Who do you think this is for? Describe them in your own words.**
*(Paragraph)*

[Why: If their description doesn't match who you think it's for, your design is misaligned with their read of it.]

### Question 14
**What's one thing you wish this app did that it doesn't?**
*(Paragraph)*

### Question 15
**What's one thing it does that you'd cut?**
*(Paragraph)*

[Why: Asking what to remove is harder and more useful than asking what to add. Most people only suggest additions.]

### Question 16
**On a scale of 1-10, how likely are you to recommend this to another Swiftie in recovery once it's ready?**
*(Linear scale 1-10)*

[Why: This is the classic Net Promoter question. Anything below an 8 from your most loyal members means real refinement is needed.]

### Question 17
**Anything else you want to say? Even if it doesn't fit the questions above. Even if it's small. Even if it's hard to say.**
*(Paragraph)*

[Why: People often save their truest feedback for the open box at the end. Always include it.]

---

## After Each Tester Submits

Read their responses within 24 hours. Don't reply with defensive context — they don't need to know why something is the way it is, they need to feel heard.

Send a short thank-you note. Something like: "Just read your feedback. Thank you for the time and the honesty. The piece about [specific thing they said] really lands. I'm taking it in."

Don't promise to fix anything specific yet. Wait until you have all five responses and can spot patterns.

---

## When You Have All Five Responses

Look for:

- **Things 3+ people noticed independently.** Those are the real signals. Anything one person mentioned might just be them.
- **Differences between question 5 (sounded like Julianne?) and question 8 (would you actually open it?)** People might say it sounds like you but still wouldn't use it — that's a different problem to solve than voice tuning.
- **The wishlist patterns.** If three people independently say "I wish I could see who else is online" or "I wish there was a way to mark a moment as a win," those are real product signals.
- **The specific words testers use.** If they describe the companion in language that matches your brand voice ("she," "Julianne," "the program"), you're winning. If they describe it in tech terms ("it," "the bot," "the AI"), you're not there yet.
